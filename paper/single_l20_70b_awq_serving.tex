\begin{abstract}
Serving 70B-class open-weight language models is commonly associated with 80GB accelerators or multi-GPU inference, yet many practical deployments are constrained to smaller 48GB-class GPUs. This report evaluates whether a single NVIDIA L20 can sustain a 70B-class quantized model under a fixed production-style serving workload. We serve Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct-AWQ using vLLM 0.8.5.post1 with AWQ Marlin on one L20 GPU and measure throughput, latency, reliability, and GPU-board energy over both short sweeps and a 24-hour soak. Under a fixed workload of approximately 512 input tokens, 256 output tokens, and concurrency 10, the system completed 36,740 out of 36,740 requests over 86,412.6 seconds, sustaining 108.84 output tokens/s with p95 time-to-first-token (TTFT) of 6.61s, p95 end-to-end latency of 23.54s, no request failures, and no CUDA OOM/error signatures in the vLLM log. GPU power sampled every 10 seconds yielded an estimated 330.15W average board power, 7.92 kWh GPU-board energy, 0.330 output tokens/J, and 1.008 total tokens/J.

The result should be interpreted narrowly: it is strong evidence that a single L20 can sustain one carefully configured Qwen2.5-72B AWQ serving workload, not evidence of lossless quality retention, long-context high-concurrency behavior, or general production SLA coverage. Relative to the closest public 512-input/256-output/10-concurrent Qwen 2.5 72B Q4 table we found, this L20 run is competitive with and above the published single-GPU figures, while NVIDIA's optimized NIM profiles for Qwen2.5 72B on L20 are multi-GPU FP8 configurations. We conclude that single-L20 70B-class AWQ serving is feasible and measurable, and that the next necessary step is quality-retention evaluation against a high-quality FP16/BF16 or trusted external baseline.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Large open-weight language models in the 70B-parameter class occupy a useful point in the model landscape: they are substantially stronger than many 7B--14B systems, can be self-hosted, and are small enough to be realistic for organizations that cannot deploy frontier-scale clusters. The operational difficulty is that these models are usually discussed in the context of 80GB GPUs or multi-GPU servers. A 48GB GPU has enough memory for 4-bit weights, but whether it can sustain useful serving behavior over time depends on more than static model fit. KV-cache allocation, batching policy, quantization kernels, prompt/output shape, request concurrency, and thermal or memory-fragmentation effects all determine whether the result is an engineering demo or a repeatable service.

This report studies a concrete question:

\begin{quote}
Can one NVIDIA L20 sustain a 70B-class quantized LLM under a fixed, externally comparable serving workload for 24 hours?
\end{quote}

We answer this question empirically for Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct-AWQ, served through vLLM with the AWQ Marlin kernel. We focus on sustained serving behavior rather than model quality. The model family itself is already well documented: Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct is a 72.7B-parameter instruction-tuned model with grouped-query attention and long-context support, and the Qwen team publishes both model cards and speed benchmarks for several runtimes and quantization modes \cite{qwen25_model_card,qwen25_speed_benchmark,qwen25_report}. The contribution of this report is not a new model or quantization method. The contribution is a transparent, reproducible measurement of a constrained single-GPU deployment point that is often discussed informally but rarely documented with day-long stability and energy numbers.

\paragraph{Contributions.}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item We report a 24-hour fixed-shape serving soak of Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct-AWQ on a single NVIDIA L20, including success rate, TTFT, end-to-end latency, output throughput, request throughput, GPU-board energy, and OOM/error checks.
  \item We provide the exact serving configuration, workload shape, sampling controls, energy formula, and measurement scope.
  \item We compare the result against public Qwen 2.5 72B/Q4 serving numbers, Qwen official speed benchmarks, and NVIDIA NIM hardware profile guidance, while explicitly separating comparable aggregate-throughput data from non-comparable single-request speed tables.
  \item We identify the limits of the claim and the experiments required before making quality-retention or production-SLA claims.
\end{itemize}

\section{Background}

\subsection{Why 70B on 48GB Is Nontrivial}

Weight-only 4-bit quantization can reduce a 70B-class model's weight footprint enough to fit inside a 48GB GPU. Static fit, however, does not imply sustained serving capacity. During autoregressive decoding, memory is also consumed by KV cache, runtime buffers, scheduler state, CUDA graphs or eager-mode buffers, and fragmentation. The available KV-cache budget depends on the selected context length, maximum sequence count, and batching policy. A service that fits one request at a short context length may fail at longer context, and a service that survives a short benchmark can still degrade under a day-long stream of requests.

This distinction matters for user-facing claims. ``Runs on a single L20'' can mean a one-request smoke test, a low-throughput interactive endpoint, a high-throughput batched endpoint, or a stable day-long service. These are different claims. This report targets the third and fourth meanings under a fixed 512/256 serving shape.

\subsection{vLLM, Continuous Batching, and PagedAttention}

vLLM is a high-throughput serving engine built around efficient KV-cache management and continuous batching. Its PagedAttention paper reports 2--4$\times$ throughput improvements over prior serving systems under comparable latency conditions \cite{vllm_pagedattention}. NVIDIA GenAI-Perf and vLLM serving benchmarks both emphasize the same core serving metrics: output token throughput, request throughput, TTFT, inter-token latency, and end-to-end request latency \cite{genai_perf_docs,vllm_bench_serve}. We follow this metric vocabulary.

Continuous batching is central to interpreting our result. The per-request decode speed in the 24-hour run is only about 11--15 tokens/s, but the aggregate service produces 108.84 output tokens/s because multiple requests are decoded concurrently. Therefore, this result should not be described as a single-user decode speed. It is aggregate server throughput at concurrency 10.

\subsection{AWQ and Marlin}

Activation-aware Weight Quantization (AWQ) is a post-training weight-only quantization approach that protects salient channels identified through activation statistics, enabling 4-bit deployment while preserving much of the model behavior \cite{awq}. Marlin is a mixed-precision FP16$\times$INT4 inference kernel designed to recover close to the ideal speedup of 4-bit weight compression over useful batch sizes; the Marlin paper reports that integration with vLLM can improve end-to-end inference speed \cite{marlin}. Our evaluated stack uses vLLM's AWQ Marlin path (\texttt{awq\_marlin}), which is important because a generic 4-bit model file and an optimized 4-bit serving kernel are not equivalent deployment configurations.

\section{Experimental Setup}

\subsection{Hardware and Software}

The test machine exposed one NVIDIA L20 GPU with approximately 46.1GB visible VRAM through `nvidia-smi'. The model weights were stored locally on the remote host. The serving environment used:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Model: Qwen/Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct-AWQ.
  \item Runtime: vLLM 0.8.5.post1.
  \item Quantization path: AWQ Marlin (\texttt{\detokenize{--quantization awq_marlin}}).
  \item Serving API: OpenAI-compatible \texttt{/v1/chat/completions}.
  \item PyTorch/CUDA stack: PyTorch 2.6.0 + CUDA 12.4 runtime in the vLLM environment.
\end{itemize}

The primary service configuration was:

\begin{verbatim}
vllm serve /home/hhai/models/Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct-AWQ \
  --host 0.0.0.0 --port 8001 \
  --served-model-name qwen72b-awq-l20 \
  --quantization awq_marlin \
  --dtype half \
  --max-model-len 1024 \
  --gpu-memory-utilization 0.98 \
  --max-num-seqs 48 \
  --max-num-batched-tokens 4096 \
  --enforce-eager \
  --swap-space 1 \
  --disable-log-requests \
  --trust-remote-code
\end{verbatim}

The short-context configuration intentionally caps \texttt{max\_model\_len} at 1024. This is not a long-context service profile; it is a high-throughput fixed-shape profile. Separate 4096- and 8192-context single-concurrency validation runs are reported in Section~\ref{sec:long_context}.

\subsection{Workload}

The externally comparable fixed-shape benchmark used:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Prompt set: 128 unique prompts.
  \item Raw tokenizer prompt length: 498 tokens.
  \item Server-side prompt usage after chat formatting: approximately 527 tokens on average.
  \item Output control: \texttt{max\_tokens=256}, \texttt{min\_tokens=256}, \texttt{ignore\_eos=true}.
  \item Sampling: \texttt{temperature=0}.
  \item Streaming: enabled, with \texttt{stream\_options.include\_usage=true} to collect prompt and completion token counts from server-side usage metadata.
\end{itemize}

The 24-hour soak reused this fixed-shape workload at concurrency 10. This shape was selected because it aligns closely with the public GigaGPU 2026 benchmark methodology, which reports 512-token prompts, 256-token generations, and 10 concurrent users for Qwen 2.5 72B Q4 on several GPUs \cite{gigagpu_2026}.

\subsection{Metrics}

We use standard serving metrics:

\begin{align}
\mathrm{success\_rate} &= \frac{N_\mathrm{successful}}{N_\mathrm{total}}, \\
\mathrm{output\_tok/s} &= \frac{\sum_i \mathrm{output\_tokens}_i}{T_\mathrm{benchmark}}, \\
\mathrm{req/s} &= \frac{N_\mathrm{completed}}{T_\mathrm{benchmark}}.
\end{align}

TTFT is the time from request submission to the first streamed token. End-to-end latency is the time from request submission to final response. Inter-token latency (ITL) and time per output token (TPOT) describe the decode phase after the first token. These definitions are consistent with NVIDIA GenAI-Perf's metric descriptions and vLLM's serving benchmark output \cite{genai_perf_docs,vllm_bench_serve}.

Energy is computed from `nvidia-smi' GPU board-power samples:

\begin{align}
E_\mathrm{J} &= \int P(t) \, dt, \\
\mathrm{output\_tok/J} &= \frac{\sum_i \mathrm{output\_tokens}_i}{E_\mathrm{J}}, \\
\mathrm{total\_tok/J} &= \frac{\sum_i (\mathrm{prompt\_tokens}_i + \mathrm{output\_tokens}_i)}{E_\mathrm{J}}.
\end{align}

The implementation uses trapezoidal integration over the sampled power trace. Power was sampled approximately every 10 seconds. These are GPU-board energy estimates, not full-system wall-power measurements; CPU, DRAM, storage, power-supply losses, and cooling overhead are not included.

The most comparable public table is GigaGPU's April 2026 benchmark, which states that it used vLLM 0.8.x with continuous batching, 512-token prompts, 256-token generations, and 10 concurrent users \cite{gigagpu_2026}. Under that shape, their Qwen 2.5 72B Q4 entries report 32 tok/s on RTX 3090, 58--82 tok/s on RTX 5090 variants, and 45 tok/s on RTX 6000 Pro. Our L20 result is above that range and adds a day-long stability measurement. This does not prove that L20 is generally faster than those GPUs; it shows that this particular L20/AWQ-Marlin/vLLM configuration is highly competitive under the tested shape.

The Qwen official speed benchmark is valuable for sanity checking, but it is not the same test. Qwen reports single-request 72B AWQ vLLM speed on two A100 80GB GPUs at batch size 1 and long 2048-token outputs, with speeds from 44.30 tok/s at input length 1 to 30.02 tok/s at input length 30,720 \cite{qwen25_speed_benchmark}. Those numbers measure a different operating point: high-quality multi-GPU single-request speed and long-context behavior, not aggregate 10-concurrent serving on a 48GB card.

NVIDIA NIM's supported-model table is also instructive. Its optimized Qwen2.5 72B Instruct L20 FP8 profiles list 4 or 8 L20 GPUs, while A100 SXM BF16 profiles list 4 or 8 GPUs depending on profile \cite{nvidia_nim_supported}. Our result therefore demonstrates a different deployment path: single-L20 AWQ Marlin outside the conservative optimized FP8 profile matrix.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Why the Result Is Strong}

The strongest aspect of the result is not the absolute throughput alone. It is the agreement between the short concurrency-10 screening run and the day-long run. A short run at 108.70 output tok/s could be a transient peak. A 24-hour run at 108.84 output tok/s with zero failures and no OOM/error signatures is evidence of sustained stability under that exact workload.

The result is also practically meaningful because it uses a single 48GB-class GPU. Many public 70B serving configurations rely on 80GB GPUs or multi-GPU tensor parallelism. A single-GPU deployment avoids inter-GPU communication, simplifies scheduling, and reduces operational complexity. The tradeoff is that model length, concurrency, and latency must be tightly controlled.

\subsection{Throughput Versus Latency}

The service is throughput-oriented. At concurrency 10, p95 TTFT is 6.61s and p95 latency is 23.54s for 256-token outputs. This may be acceptable for batch generation, background summarization, asynchronous agents, or internal processing pipelines. It is less appropriate for low-latency interactive chat where users expect the first token in under a second. Reducing concurrency, shortening inputs or outputs, enabling a different scheduler profile, or using a smaller model would improve latency at the cost of aggregate throughput.

\subsection{The Role of Context Length}

The main result uses \texttt{max\_model\_len} set to 1024. This is a deliberate throughput configuration. Qwen2.5-72B supports much longer contexts, but long context shifts the bottleneck by increasing prefill cost and KV-cache pressure. The 4K and 8K single-concurrency checks show feasibility, not throughput. A separate paper-quality long-context claim should use fixed 4K/8K/16K workloads, concurrency sweeps, and independent soak tests.

\subsection{What This Does Not Prove}

The result does not prove that the AWQ model is lossless relative to FP16/BF16. It does not prove that all 70B models fit or perform similarly on L20. It does not prove production reliability across arbitrary prompts, tool use, long outputs, multi-tenant traffic, or long-context retrieval workloads. It also does not prove full-system energy efficiency, because we measured GPU board power rather than wall power.

These limitations are not cosmetic; they define the next experiments. A strong systems result becomes a stronger research result when paired with quality retention and broader deployment ablations.

\section{Threats to Validity}

\paragraph{Single model and quantization path.}
All main results use Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct-AWQ with AWQ Marlin. GPTQ, GGUF, FP8, EXL2, and other 70B-class models may behave differently.

\paragraph{Fixed workload.}
The 24-hour workload uses approximately 512 input tokens and exactly 256 output tokens with concurrency 10. Real traffic has variable prompt lengths, output lengths, retries, user disconnects, tool calls, and burstiness.

\paragraph{Quality not evaluated.}
The report measures serving capacity and stability. It does not evaluate MMLU, CMMLU, GSM8K, MT-Bench, LongBench, or domain-specific quality retention against an FP16/BF16 baseline.

\paragraph{Energy scope.}
Energy is GPU-board energy from \texttt{nvidia-smi}, not wall energy. Board-power sampling is useful for regression but incomplete for cost or emissions accounting.

\paragraph{No multi-run confidence intervals.}
The 24-hour run is a single day-long run. Repeating the run across days, hosts, driver versions, and ambient conditions would improve statistical confidence.

\section{Future Work}

The next stage should prioritize quality retention:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Run MMLU/MMLU-Pro, CMMLU/C-Eval, GSM8K/MATH, MT-Bench/Arena-Hard, and LongBench/LongBench v2.
  \item Compare the AWQ candidate against a high-quality full-precision Qwen2.5-72B endpoint or a trusted hosted reference. A single L20 cannot host that baseline locally.
  \item Add additional 70B-class models: Llama 3.3 70B AWQ, DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-70B AWQ, and Qwen3 70B-class checkpoints if suitable quantized variants are available.
  \item Compare runtimes: vLLM, SGLang, llama.cpp/GGUF, and TensorRT-LLM where compatible.
  \item Add quantization ablations: AWQ Marlin, GPTQ Marlin, FP8 where hardware and profiles permit, and GGUF Q4 variants.
  \item Measure wall power in addition to GPU-board power, and report cost per million output tokens under realistic electricity pricing.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}

This report shows that a single NVIDIA L20 can sustain Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct-AWQ serving for 24 hours under a fixed 512-input/256-output, concurrency-10 workload when configured with vLLM 0.8.5.post1 and AWQ Marlin. The system completed 36,740 requests with 100\% success, sustained 108.84 output tokens/s, consumed an estimated 7.92 kWh of GPU-board energy, and showed no OOM/error signatures. The result is strong evidence for a specific single-L20 70B-class throughput-serving configuration. It should be framed as sustained serving evidence, not as a lossless-quality or general-production claim. The most important next step is to pair this systems result with rigorous quality-retention evaluation.
